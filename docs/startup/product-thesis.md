# Product Thesis

## Mission

Build the entertainment platform that transforms written stories into living visual worlds.

## Initial Customer

Independent authors and web-novel writers who have a compelling story and audience but cannot afford a traditional comic, animation, or film production team.

Secondary customers after validation:

- small publishers testing adaptation potential;
- comic and animation studios accelerating pre-production;
- rights holders evaluating back-catalog stories;
- readers who want text and visuals in one experience.

## Problem

Individual image and video models can create impressive outputs, but a story adaptation is a long-running production system. It must preserve characters, locations, props, clothing, tone, chronology, camera language, rights, and approvals across many scenes.

Authors also lack an affordable way to test whether their story can become a visual franchise without surrendering creative control or funding a full studio.

## Wedge Product

A human-directed service and software workflow that turns one licensed or public-domain chapter into an 8–15-panel premium illustrated chapter or vertical comic.

The author controls:

- canonical character and world details;
- visual references and style;
- scene selection and adaptation;
- revision and final approval;
- publication and rights decisions.

## Value Proposition

For authors: a studio-quality adaptation test at a fraction of traditional time and cost, without losing control of the story.

For readers: a faithful visual reading experience that preserves the original text while making characters, environments, and action easier to experience.

For the company: a repeatable way to discover promising stories, improve production technology, build audience data, and earn options to expand successful properties.

## Business Model Sequence

### Stage 1: Production service

Charge authors or small publishers for a finished illustrated pilot chapter. Use fixed-scope packages and human creative direction.

### Stage 2: Revenue-share publishing

Select promising stories, license limited adaptation rights, publish visual chapters, and share revenue with authors.

### Stage 3: IP incubation

Use engagement data to invest in successful properties and expand them into animation, games, merchandise, or film.

### Stage 4: Platform

Offer the production operating system, author marketplace, reader platform, and cross-format asset pipeline.

## Defensible Moat

The moat is not a single image model. Model vendors will change.

The durable system is:

- a structured, versioned Story Bible;
- entity and relationship memory;
- a continuity graph across scenes and formats;
- human approvals and production provenance;
- rights, territory, term, and derivative-work metadata;
- reusable character, location, prop, and style assets;
- engagement data connecting stories to expansion decisions;
- author and creator relationships.

## Rights Principles

- Use public-domain stories for internal engineering benchmarks.
- Obtain written adaptation permission before using modern copyrighted stories.
- Record rights holder, permitted formats, territory, term, exclusivity, revenue share, and AI-use permissions.
- Never treat a submitted manuscript as permission to train a general model.
- Preserve source attribution, production history, and author approvals.
- Consult qualified counsel before commercial licensing.

## Explicit Non-Goals for the First Product

- full movies or television episodes;
- training a proprietary foundation model;
- an open consumer “generate anything” tool;
- a public marketplace;
- automated publication without human approval;
- acquiring broad rights before product-market evidence exists.

## Three Decisions That Matter Now

1. Target independent authors first, not every reader, studio, and publisher.
2. Sell a consistent illustrated pilot chapter, not a promise of an AI movie.
3. Own the story/continuity/rights workflow while treating generation models as replaceable suppliers.
